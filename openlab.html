Open Lab

Open Lab is a free-to-use makerspace on the University of Pittsburgh’s campus. It offers a variety of fabrication equipment and workshops to students, faculty, and staff. I was asked to redesign the website to make it more user friendly, as members frequently had trouble navigating through it.

Time

1 week

Tools

Figma, Adobe Illustrator

Deliverable

Design System

Context

The main purpose of the Open Lab website is for people to learn about how to become a member. Instead, students and faculty were coming into the physical location to ask staff directly. The common opener was: “I went to the website, but...”

Myself, the head of Open Lab, and three other staff members sat down to review the problems. Eventually, we outlined two main goals.

  • Differentiate student and faculty needs

  • Remove bloat and outdated information

01. Messy User Journey

First Impressions

The biggest problem we identified was the confusing landing section of the website. Newcomers to the site were instantly redirected to different links. This could work for someone who wanted an in-depth overview, but it was frustrating for people who wanted simple questions answered.

Empathizing with New Members

To better understand what a clear user pathway could look like, I considered the unique pain points that students and faculty could have. Since I've worked at Open Lab for over a year, I was already familiar with many of the complaints people had about the website. Many users would view it out of curiosity, or because they had a specific project they wanted to create, but would often get lost in the abundance of links and redirects.

Solution: Focused Purpose

To remedy this, I distilled the site's purpose into two main actions : how students can become a member, and how faculty can get consultations. This action-based approach would keep people on the site for longer and direct them to exactly what they needed to know. By using anchors, they wouldn't instantly be redirected to another window.

02. Information Bloat

Old Content

The second biggest problem was the outdated information. Since the site hadn't been updated for years, new pieces of equipment were nowhere to be found.

Additionally, clicking on the technology links brought users to a lengthy FAQ page. This was heavily redundant, since the Canvas page covered all these details in a better, more modern fashion. In short - the additional pages conflated the purpose of the website with Open Lab's Canvas page, and as my stakeholders wanted things as simple as possible, we decided to cut these extra pages.

Solution: Show What's Necessary

To account for the new additions and the succinct design goals, I then reduced this section to visual badges of each available service. By doing this, we could afford more scalability and display everything at a glance. It's a better portrayal of the large variety of technology at members' disposal, and cuts out any overlap with the Canvas page.

03. Design System

Atomic Design Components

My main challenge for the final design was the limited amount of time I had. Due to this constraint, most of my energy went toward creating modular components that could be easily rearranged. Since I wasn't in direct contact with the development team, this would be helpful for ensuring certain aspects were intact, while granting them the freedom to do as they pleased.

Layout Mockups

To convey a sense of what the site could look like, I created two layouts that mixed and match the components. With this design system, and with the clear user paths, most sections could be put in any order and assets easily moved around. I was able to provide the development team a proof of concept while leaving room for iteration and flexibility. This also satisfied the stakeholders' request to keep things as simple as possible, leaving only the essential components of the site.

Outcome

The final designs were approved by the University, and are currently under development.
