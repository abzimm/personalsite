Perch


Metamodern Media was the final assignment for my Fall 2023 Projects in Digital Composition class. We were tasked with creating an interactive kiosk display based on our chosen topic. Since the projects would be put on public display, they were meant to be as accessible and user-friendly as possible.

01. What makes Perch stand out?

Competetive Analysis

Since focus apps are such a popular concept, I tested 4 of the most popular ones of the Play Store to see which features were most popular. I outlined what I liked and disliked about each app. My personal experience always felt discouraging - it felt like the app was working against me, instead of working with me and my personal needs.

User Research

Based on my competetive analysis, I created a Qualtrics survey that was taken by 10 of my peers. The questions focused on their feelings about focus tools, the effectiveness of common features, and what their ideal focus tool would look like. I was able to gather some interesting insights:

  • There's still a gap in the market-90% of participants had tried a focus app before, but found it NOT helpful.

  • There's no one-size-fits-all solution. When asked about their ideal focus tool, there wasn't a consistent answer: “Something to relax my brain.” “Study music, soft alarms for distractions, strict app blocking.” “Time tracking / blocking, maybe internal playlists, incentives to continue working.”

    • Solution: Focus with Friends

    But there was one answer in particular that stuck out to me: "...the sense that other people around me are also completing tasks. As a college student, studying in public with my friends has always been helpful - the "body doubling" effect enforces accountability in a low stress way. Also, other focus apps lacked a social angle. With this in mind, I wanted my design to stand out by allowing the user to see others focusing.

    By displaying the user's friends as birds "perching", the social aspect is reinforced without being overly distracting. It indicates only timers and names, and customizing the bird allows for self expression.

02. How do I account for different needs?

User Personas + User Flow

Based on my survey answers, I created two distinct user personas. The stand-out insight was that people had diverse needs and wants for a focus app, so my design should be low stakes, flexible, and adaptable.

I created a user flow to get a better sense of how this would play out. This clarified the features further, and made me think about aspects like editing a session or ending it early.

Solution: Customizing the Focus Session

When reviewing the disparity between current focus apps and my survey responses, I reached a key conclusion: currently, users had to adapt to the app, not the other way around. I felt like this was antithetical to creating a good productive environment - any friction should be opted into, not an automatic design feature. Out of the most popular features, I chose to incorporate app blocking and varying levels of "distraction barriers", as only the user could know what they wanted blocked and how they wanted it blocked.

Solution: Gentle Reinforcement

Another big divide was whether or not people enjoyed gamification features, like getting points and rewards. I decided to have a non-invasive shop - it wouldn't take away attention from the app's purpose, but it would provide a relaxed incentive, and played nicely into showing off to your friends.

03. Prototype + Final Design

Usability Testing

I created an interactive prototype in Figma to begin blocking out the final product. I incorporated my my design insights by prioritizing social and customizable features. Then, I asked 5 of the original survey respondents to accomplish tasks and give their opinions.

The overall impression was positive:

  • Having a more written-out onboarding would be nice, particularly for accessibility and screen readers.

  • All respondents would use it in real life, and felt that "perching" with others was a definite motivator. One reported that the app was "still on their mind" after the testing, as they wanted the same degree of freedom in current apps being used.

  • All respondents wanted to give their bird a fun hat.

Hifi Prototype

I assembled a moodboard and style guide for the overall look and feel of the app. I wanted it to feel calming and serene, so I looked to other zen-like apps for inspiration. With my styling, I created various components and icons to assemble the final design.
